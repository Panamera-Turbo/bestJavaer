This is one of the most thought-provoking trends: the rejuvenation of the value of white-collar work and the rise of "super individuals".



This is not to say that the white collar class will disappear, but rather that jobs that are highly dependent on information processing and processization are facing challenges. Back in 2023, when ChatGPT was first born, we thought AI was just a novel tool. By today, 2026, looking around, you'll find that if your core job is just to sort out forms, write formatted reports, write basic codes, then even in the skyscrapers of the CBD, the alternative job is actually getting taller.



The scarcity of intellectual labour, which had been considered complex in the past, is diminishing today, when the cost of computing has fallen sharply. Work previously done by a team may now be carried out by one person, with several AI smarts. This may lead to streamlining of some intermediate management levels.



The opportunity for the future lies in whether you can be the man who drives the tool, defines the value.



Here's a perception that needs to be updated. Embrace AI, doesn't mean everyone has to be a programmer. In future AI applications, the threshold will be significantly reduced, and what is really scarce is your ** logical build-up capacity, aesthetic judgement and ultimate decision-making power.**



The real dividend may flow to "super individuals" with unique knowledge and enforcement power. In the past, entrepreneurship may require teams, premises and financing. In the coming years, there will be an increasing number of models in which one person is an efficient company. The flexibility and efficiency of this model pose new challenges to traditional business patterns.



So it might be possible to revisit the Great Factory Halo. Large companies have platform advantages, but for individuals, true security comes from whether you can turn industry experience into a standardized process that can be implemented by AI, thereby creating independent user values.



If you're a consultant, can you train a digital assistant to analyze your data? If you're a designer, can you build an automated process underlying design? That is where we should think.

Finally, we inevitably have to talk about the reality that the wealth structure of society may be a K-type divide.



Over the next 5 to 10 years, some of those who hold core assets (e. g., high-quality equity, scarce IP, unique knowledge) may continue to grow in wealth, while others receive basic security through the provision of basic services. The most stressful is probably the group in the middle - those of you and me who rely on common skills to work in relatively comfortable areas.



If we do not proactively seek a leap in capacity, we can slide to a more price-dependent dimension of competition. Because your skills are not unique enough for top-level design or cheap enough for bottom-level execution.



The future logic of personal investment and development therefore needs to be adjusted. Core assets are no longer real estate, but your ** capacity to continuously create value** - healthy bodies, scarce skills, influential personal brands, deep industry awareness.

In the era of division, two strategies are relatively clear:



1. **Deeply done, top of service**: to serve a small minority that is not price-sensitive but highly critical of quality and experience. It requires you to have the highest professional competence and level of resources.

 

2. ** Wide and inclusive of the public**: products or services of excess value are provided to the widest possible range of users through extreme efficiency. This requires strong supply chain management capabilities and scale effects.

 

What is most important is the intermediate status of those who have neither brand premiums nor cost advantages. Such operations will face enormous survival pressures in the future.
