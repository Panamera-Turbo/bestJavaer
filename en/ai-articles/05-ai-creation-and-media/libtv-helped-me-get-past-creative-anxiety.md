# LibTV Helped Me Get Past My Creative Anxiety

I used to think that to make a video, I had to spend 3 days learning Stable Diffusion and then 2 days learning editing. After I finished studying, my enthusiasm was gone.

But this time, it only took me 30 minutes to eat breakfast and I was done. All thanks to this thing called LibTV.

But to be honest, there is no point in just talking about speed, because there are too many tools on the market that claim to be one-click generation.

I have experienced some video platforms, and I found that when it comes to video creation, I will eventually return to the generator, canvas and nodes, because these three points are the soul.

Although video creation is no longer something that can only be done by professionals, the process is still basically the same: writing scripts, arranging shots, unifying characters, and reworking the screen. And as long as one of the small details is modified, it may affect the entire workflow.

**Agent is easy to complete the first step, but the real difficulty is to deliver the video in its entirety.**
I experienced it for a while and I think LibTV did a very good job with the whole process.

It has greatly lowered the threshold for video creation. Next, I will talk to you about why I say this.

This is the official website of LibTV, you can go directly to https://www.liblib.tv/

---

When it comes to video creation, I am a novice like everyone else, so I will share with you the process from the initial topic selection to the final production of the video, hoping to give you a better understanding of this tool.

During the recent World Cup, France and Spain have just entered the semi-finals. This is a focus game. So my first idea was to make a trailer for the game between France and Spain.

I first roughly described the style I wanted, and then used LibTV’s Agent mode to give me many styles to choose from. I chose the movie epic style, combined it with the historical background of the two teams, and then uploaded two pairs of portraits of several core players to start generating a production script.

Throughout the experience, I found that LibTV has two modes, one is workflow and the other is storyboard.

The workflow is easy to understand, it is a complete process from creating assets -> generating storyboard video -> compositing the final video.

The storyboard is laid out according to the story background of the video creation, which is equivalent to directly editing the story script corresponding to the storyboard. I think this mode is very convenient.

At this point, I have my first feelings about LibTV:

The Almighty Agent first breaks down my ideas into scripts, characters, scenes and shots, and then arranges subsequent generation tasks. I don’t have to study which model to choose at each step right from the beginning, which is very friendly to novices.

The workflow lets me know where each step is going, and the storyboard lets me see each shot from the perspective of the entire story.

The two modes can be switched at any time, one manages the production process and the other manages the narrative style.

If there is a problem with a player's portrait, camera position or movement, I can go back to the corresponding assets and storyboards and modify them individually without having to tear down the entire video and start over. The images, videos and sound models behind them can also be scheduled directly in the same project.

Of course, it’s impossible to watch the video every time. I also reworked several areas such as player consistency and storyboard connection. But being able to pinpoint specific lens changes and doing it all over again are completely different things.

**I think LibTV reduces the difficulty of finishing a video.**

After completing this case, I roughly understood the value of Skill for video creation.

To put it bluntly, Skill is to preserve the creative methods that have been verified by others. I don’t need to learn how to be a comedy screenwriter first, and I don’t need to start with a blank prompt. It will first ask my preferences and then give script and storyboard prompts.

---

After completing three cases, I have a clearer understanding of LibTV.

The World Cup trailer allowed me to experience the full-featured Agent, workflow and storyboard; "The Strongest Interviewer" allowed me to directly reuse the Skills that others had developed; and "Rolo at Home" allowed me to create the Skill myself and then use it to complete the film.

These three cases correspond to three completely different creative methods.

You can give your ideas directly to Agent, and let it break down the script, prepare assets, generate storyboards, and then combine it into a video.

You can also find a mature method from Skill Hub and follow it step by step.

If you already have your own creative habits, you can also turn this set of habits into Skills and reuse them next time.

**In my opinion, the most valuable thing about LibTV is that it puts Agent, workflow, storyboard and Skill together so that an idea can be turned into a film.**
Of course, typing one sentence doesn't guarantee success for all shots at once. Character consistency, lens connection and final aesthetic judgment still need to be checked by people.

But for a video creation novice like me, I basically wanted to give up after seeing the long process of writing scripts, doing storyboards, preparing materials, selecting models, and editing into films. Now I can at least start working on it and come back to specific assets and lens modifications when I encounter problems.

**Tools lower the threshold, and whether the final film is good or not still depends on what you want to express.**
